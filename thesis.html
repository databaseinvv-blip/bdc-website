From Watt to Byte.

Why Sarawak's clean energy advantage underwrites the next decade of digital infrastructure and the case for a place-based, long-horizon fund built to deploy along that conversion.

Borneo Development Capital / The firm's founding thesis / Filed under: Strategy, Sarawak Development

Sarawak's natural endowment is, in this decade, being converted into clean electricity. That electricity, in turn, is becoming the foundation for the digital infrastructure of the next economy. We invest along this conversion. We call it the move from Watt to Byte.

For most of its modern history, Sarawak's economic story has been told through its raw materials. Timber, oil and gas, palm, and minerals supplied an outsized share of state revenue and built much of the region's early industrial base. The story of the next decade will be different. It will be told through energy and intelligence, and through the infrastructure that connects the two.

The state's published development blueprint, the Sarawak Development Strategy 2030, sets a target of growing state GDP from approximately RM150 billion to RM282 billion by 2030. A meaningful share of that growth is attributable to two sectors that are now structurally linked: renewable energy generation, and the digital infrastructure that low-carbon, low-cost power makes possible. We believe this conversion, from energy abundance to digital capacity, defines the most consequential investment opportunity in Southeast Asia over the next ten years.

The Watt layer

Sarawak's energy story begins with geography. The state covers an area larger than Peninsular Malaysia, with abundant rainfall, mountainous terrain that supports large-scale hydroelectric generation, and a stable equatorial climate. These features have already enabled the development of major hydropower assets, with installed capacity that places Sarawak among the most electricity-rich jurisdictions per capita in the region.

The state is now layering additional renewable capacity on top of that hydro base. New solar projects, hybrid systems combining hydro with solar and storage, and emerging energy-storage infrastructure are extending what was already a clean energy advantage into something approaching a regional leadership position. The current trajectory is for Sarawak to function as a net exporter of green electricity within the decade, supplying clean power not only to its own industries but to neighbouring jurisdictions and, increasingly, to data-intensive industries seeking decarbonised operations.

For an investor, the relevant feature is not the energy itself but the cost structure it produces. Power in Sarawak is cheap by regional standards, getting cheaper as renewable capacity expands, and stable in supply because of the diversification across hydro, solar, and storage. That combination is rare. It creates a structural cost advantage for any industry whose primary input is electricity.

The case for Sarawak is not about a region. It is about conviction, and what kind of capital is willing to back a place over a horizon long enough to see it through.

The Byte layer

Cheap, clean, reliable power is the primary input to one of the fastest-growing industries of the next decade: data centres and the broader digital infrastructure they enable. The economics here are straightforward. Data centres are among the most energy-intensive facilities in the modern economy. Their site selection is governed by three factors: the cost of power, the carbon profile of that power, and the political and climatic stability of the location. Sarawak now offers all three at scale.

The state's natural cooling conditions reduce the auxiliary power required to operate large-scale facilities. The available land base is substantial and largely undeveloped, allowing for purpose-built campuses rather than retrofitted urban locations. The connectivity infrastructure, including submarine cables and regional fibre links, has been steadily expanding. And the policy environment, anchored in the SDS 2030 framework, has explicitly positioned digital infrastructure as a priority sector.

What this produces, over time, is a digital infrastructure cluster. The cluster begins with the data centres themselves. It extends into the secondary infrastructure they require: cooling systems, backup power, fibre connectivity, on-site renewable capacity. It extends further into the tertiary economy: the engineering, the operations, the talent base, the supplier networks. And it extends, in the longer run, into the higher-value digital industries that locate themselves near reliable compute: artificial intelligence research, financial services, content infrastructure, smart-city systems.

The conversion in numbers

The state's own projection of the economic contribution from this energy-to-digital conversion sets a useful frame. Across the official development strategy:

These are state-published targets, not BDC projections. We cite them because they signal the direction of policy and capital allocation, not because they constitute a forecast. What matters for an investor is the consistency of policy direction, the commitment of public infrastructure spending, and the scale of the addressable opportunity. On all three dimensions, the published evidence is unambiguous.

What this means for capital

A thesis is only useful if it produces investable conclusions. Ours does. Three observations follow from the Watt-to-Byte view.

First, the layered nature of the opportunity demands a multi-strategy fund.

The energy layer is dominated by long-duration, contractual, infrastructure-style cashflows. These suit defensive, yield-focused capital. The digital infrastructure layer requires more concentrated equity exposure, often through structured public-market positions or private growth investment. The cluster economy that emerges from both is best accessed through selective high-growth positions in operating companies. A fund focused on one layer alone captures a fraction of the available return profile. A fund built across all three captures the conversion itself.

Second, this is fundamentally a place-based opportunity, not a thematic one.

The Watt-to-Byte thesis works in Sarawak specifically because of the geography, the climate, the policy environment, and the existing infrastructure base. It does not generalise to other regions. Capital that approaches this opportunity through a sector lens, treating Sarawak as one of many possible jurisdictions, will lack the relationships and operating knowledge required to source and execute the best deals. Capital that approaches it through a place lens, building deep local presence and long-term commitment, has a meaningful and durable edge.

Third, the time horizon matters more than the entry point.

The conversion from Watt to Byte is a decade-long process, not a quarter-by-quarter trade. The infrastructure being built today produces returns through the late 2020s and 2030s. The cluster economy that emerges from it compounds over an even longer horizon. Capital deployed with patience, and structured to remain deployed through the cycle, will compound differently than capital structured for short-tenure exits. BDC is built explicitly for this horizon.

Why we believe a Sarawak-focused, place-based fund is the right structure

BDC was constituted with a single conviction: that the next decade of Sarawak's development deserves capital that understands the place. The Watt-to-Byte thesis is the most concrete expression of why. The opportunity is too distinctive to be captured by generic regional or sector funds. It requires direct relationships with the state, with the GLCs and project sponsors, with the operating businesses. It requires patience. It requires conviction that the development trajectory will be sustained.

Those things are easier to claim than to deliver. They are most credibly delivered by a firm that is, by structure, only able to win if Sarawak wins. That is the firm we set out to build.

The conversion from Watt to Byte is already underway. The capital that arrives early, stays patient, and understands the place will be best positioned to compound through it.
